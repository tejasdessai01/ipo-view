## What is an IPO?

An Initial Public Offering (IPO) is when a private company offers its shares to the public for the first time. It allows retail investors to buy shares before the company starts trading on stock exchanges like NSE and BSE.

## How to Apply for an IPO

### 1. Have a Demat Account

You need an active Demat account with a registered depository participant (DP) like Zerodha, Groww, Angel One, or any bank that offers Demat services. Your PAN card must be linked.

### 2. Check Open IPOs

Visit IPO Watch India or your broker's app to see currently open IPOs, their price bands, lot sizes, and subscription status.

### 3. Apply via UPI (Easiest Method)

Most retail investors apply through the **ASBA (Application Supported by Blocked Amount)** process using UPI:

1. Open your broker's app (Zerodha, Groww, etc.)
2. Go to the IPO section
3. Select the IPO you want to apply for
4. Enter the number of lots and your bid price
5. Enter your UPI ID
6. Approve the mandate on your UPI app (BHIM, Google Pay, PhonePe, etc.)

Your money stays in your bank account but is blocked until allotment. If you don't get shares, the block is released.

### 4. Wait for Allotment

Allotment typically happens 6-7 days after the IPO closes. You can check your allotment status on the registrar's website (KFin Technologies or Link Intime).

## Key Things to Know

- **Lot Size**: You must apply in multiples of the lot size (e.g., if lot size is 35 shares, you can apply for 35, 70, 105, etc.)
- **Cut-off Price**: For retail investors, it's usually best to bid at the cut-off price to maximize your chances of allotment
- **Maximum Investment**: Retail investors can apply for up to ₹2 lakh per IPO
- **GMP (Grey Market Premium)**: An unofficial indicator of expected listing gains. Positive GMP suggests the IPO may list above issue price

## Tips for IPO Investing

1. Read the DRHP/RHP before applying — understand the company's business and financials
2. Don't apply just because of high GMP — it can change rapidly
3. Consider the company's fundamentals: revenue growth, profitability, debt levels
4. Check subscription data — high institutional subscription is generally a positive signal
5. Have realistic expectations — not every IPO gives listing gains
